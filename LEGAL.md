### United States Federal Laws

#### Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) - 18 U.S.C. § 1030

**THE PRIMARY LAW GOVERNING COMPUTER CRIMES IN THE UNITED STATES**

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                  COMPUTER FRAUD AND ABUSE ACT (CFAA)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

What the CFAA Criminalizes:

(a)(1) - Unauthorized access to obtain classified information
PENALTY: Up to 10 years imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 20 years (repeat offense)

(a)(2) - Unauthorized access to obtain information
PENALTY: Up to 1 year imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 5 years (repeat offense if for commercial/private gain)

(a)(3) - Unauthorized access to a government computer
PENALTY: Up to 1 year imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 10 years (repeat offense)

(a)(4) - Fraud using a protected computer
PENALTY: Up to 5 years imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 10 years (repeat offense)

(a)(5) - Causing damage through unauthorized access
PENALTY: Up to 10 years imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 20 years (repeat offense)

(a)(6) - Trafficking in passwords
PENALTY: Up to 1 year imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 10 years (repeat offense)

(a)(7) - Extortion through threats to damage computers
PENALTY: Up to 5 years imprisonment (first offense)
         Up to 10 years (repeat offense)

✗ No authorization at all (hacking into systems)
✗ Exceeding authorized access (going beyond permissions)

✗ Authorization limited but exceeded
✗ Authorization from unauthorized person

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Key CFAA Definitions:**

**"Protected Computer"** = Nearly ALL computers:
- Government computers
- Financial institution computers
- Computers used in or affecting interstate commerce (essentially all internet-connected computers)
- Computers used by the federal government

**"Exceeding Authorized Access"** = Accessing a computer with authorization but obtaining information beyond what you're authorized to access

**"Damage"** = Any impairment to integrity or availability of data, programs, systems, or information

**CFAA Civil Liability:** In addition to criminal penalties, victims can sue for civil damages, including:
- Economic damages
- Equitable relief
- Attorney's fees
- Cost of investigation
- Cost of remediation

**Recent CFAA Interpretations:**
- Van Buren v. United States (2021): Limited "exceeds authorized access"
- However, many activities in this repository still clearly violate CFAA
- Terms of Service violations may or may not violate CFAA (jurisdiction-dependent)
- Criminal intent matters for severity of charges

- Disclosure of intercepted communications

### United States State Laws

**All 50 states have computer crime statutes**, which can be prosecuted IN ADDITION TO federal charges:

#### Example State Laws:

**California Penal Code § 502** (Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act)
- Unauthorized computer access
- Penalties: Up to 3 years imprisonment + fines

**New York Penal Law § 156** (Computer Tampering and Unauthorized Use)
- Computer trespass
- Penalties: Varying by degree, up to 7 years

**Texas Penal Code § 33.02** (Breach of Computer Security)

- Penalties: State jail felony to first-degree felony

**Key Point:** You can be prosecuted under BOTH federal AND state laws simultaneously. State charges may carry additional imprisonment time.

### International Laws

#### European Union

**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**
- Protects personal data of EU residents
- **Tier 1 fines:** Up to €10 million or 2% of global annual revenue (whichever is greater) - for less serious infringements
- **Tier 2 fines:** Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue (whichever is greater) - for serious infringements including unauthorized data collection and processing
- Criminal penalties vary by member state
- Applies to data processed about EU residents regardless of processor location

**NIS Directive (Network and Information Systems Directive)**
- Requires security measures for essential services and digital service providers
- Penalties for violations

**EU Cybercrime Directive (2013/40/EU)**
- Harmonizes criminal law on cyberattacks
- Member states must criminalize:
  - Illegal access to information systems
  - Illegal system interference
  - Illegal data interference
  - Production and sale of hacking tools

#### United Kingdom

**Computer Misuse Act 1990**

**Section 1: Unauthorized access to computer material**
- Penalty: Up to 2 years imprisonment + unlimited fine

**Section 2: Unauthorized access with intent to commit further offenses**
- Penalty: Up to 5 years imprisonment

**Section 3: Unauthorized acts with intent to impair computer operation**
- Penalty: Up to 10 years imprisonment

**Section 3ZA: Unauthorized acts causing serious damage**
- Penalty: Up to 14 years imprisonment
- Life imprisonment if damage threatens human welfare, national security, or economy

## 🌍 International Legal Considerations

### Cross-Border Attacks

**Key Principle**: **Where the victim is located** often determines jurisdiction, NOT where the attacker is located.

**Example Scenarios:**

**Scenario 1**: You're in the USA, attack a system in the UK
- **UK Law Applies**: Computer Misuse Act 1990
- **US Law Applies**: CFAA (using US computer systems to commit offense)
- **Result**: Subject to prosecution in BOTH jurisdictions

**Scenario 2**: You're in the UK, attack a US system
- **US Law Applies**: CFAA
- **UK Law Applies**: Computer Misuse Act (using UK internet connection)
- **Result**: Subject to prosecution in BOTH jurisdictions

**Scenario 3**: You're in the USA, attack a system in Germany
- **German Law Applies**: German Criminal Code
- **US Law Applies**: CFAA
- **EU Law Applies**: GDPR (if personal data involved)
- **Result**: Subject to prosecution in multiple jurisdictions

---

### Extradition Treaties

**The United States has extradition treaties with over 100 countries**, including:
- All European Union member states
- Canada, Mexico
- Australia, New Zealand
- Japan, South Korea
- India (limited)
- Most South American countries

**Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs):**
- Facilitate evidence sharing between countries
- Support criminal investigations
- Enable prosecution even if extradition doesn't occur

**Recent Extraditions for Computer Crimes:**
- **Lauri Love** (UK → US): Fought extradition successfully, but faced charges in both countries
- **Kim Dotcom** (NZ): Fought US extradition for years
- **Marcus Hutchins**: Detained when visiting US
- **Julian Assange**: High-profile extradition case

**Key Point**: **You CANNOT hide from computer crime prosecution by being in another country**. Modern international cooperation means prosecution is likely regardless of location.

### GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

**Applies to**:
- Processing personal data of EU residents
- **Regardless of where the processor is located**
- Even if you're in the USA, Australia, Asia, etc.

**Personal Data Includes:**
- Names, email addresses, IP addresses
- Location data
- Online identifiers (cookies, device IDs)
- Any information that can identify an individual

**GDPR Violations Relevant to Repository Content:**
- Unauthorized collection of personal data (OSINT tools)
- Processing without legal basis
- Failure to implement security measures
- Data breaches without notification
- Transferring data outside EU without safeguards

**Penalties:**
- **Tier 1**: Up to €10 million or 2% of global annual revenue (whichever is higher) - less serious infringements
- **Tier 2**: Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue (whichever is higher) - serious infringements

**Example**: A company with $1 billion revenue faces potential GDPR Tier 2 fine of **$40 million** for serious violations.

**Criminal Prosecution**: Many EU member states have criminalized GDPR violations with additional imprisonment penalties.
